The Giant Panda

A national treasure, the Giant Panda is native to China. It lives in the country's remote, central mountainous region. High bamboo forests provide a cool, damp climate, which the panda prefers. This animal typically eats at least 26 pounds of this bamboo each day to survive, but have been known to consume as much at 85 pounds of the tall green plant. Pandas have enlarged wrist bones, which function like thumbs, allowing them to pluck the thick stalks for a meal. They are gentle by nature, but weigh up to a formidible 330 pounds. Yet at birth, a baby panda is roughly the size of a stick of butter.

Why They're Important

Pandas play an important role in spreading seeds and supporting growth of vegetation in the bamboo forests where they live. There are many other animal species, some of them endangered, that benefit from this role that the panda plays in this environment, including the rare golden monkey and the crested ibis. In addition, the giant panda attracts ecotourists to the region, supporting the local economy.
 
 

Panda Facts:

  • The giant panda is an endangered species.
  • The panda cub is 1/900th the size of its mother.
  • On average, the giant panda lives 14-20 years, but can live up to 30 years in captivity.
